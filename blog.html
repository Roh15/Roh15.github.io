Why Healthcare is ML's Greatest Frontier

Exploring why I believe healthcare represents the most impactful application of machine learning, and what we need to get there...

1/3

When people ask me why I'm so focused on healthcare AI, I start with a simple thought experiment: What if every person on Earth had access to the world's best diagnostic capabilities?

Right now, medical expertise is scarce and unevenly distributed. A patient in rural areas might not have access to the same quality of diagnosis as someone in a major city. ML can change this fundamentally.

2/3

The challenges are real though. Healthcare AI isn't just about accuracy—it's about:

  • 🔒 Privacy: Patient data is sensitive. Federated learning and differential privacy aren't optional.
  • 📊 Explainability: Doctors need to understand WHY a model made a prediction.
  • ⚖️ Fairness: Models trained on biased data can perpetuate health disparities.
  • ✅ Validation: Clinical validation is rigorous for good reason.
3/3

Despite the challenges, I remain optimistic. We're seeing real progress:

Early detection of diabetic retinopathy, skin cancer screening, predicting patient deterioration... These aren't research papers anymore—they're saving lives.

The question isn't if AI will transform healthcare. It's how we ensure it benefits everyone.

The Mystery of Neural Network Learning

We've built incredibly powerful systems, but do we truly understand how they learn? Some late-night thoughts on bridging AI and neuroscience...

1/4

Here's something that keeps me up at night: We don't fully understand how our own creations learn.

We know backpropagation works. We know gradient descent finds minima. But the "why" behind generalization, the emergence of features, the lottery ticket hypothesis... there's still so much mystery.

2/4

What fascinates me is comparing artificial and biological learning:

🧬 Biological neurons: Local learning rules, sparse activations, continuous adaptation
🤖 Artificial neurons: Global error signals, dense computations, discrete training phases

Why did we diverge so much from biology? And what might we gain by converging back?

3/4

Some directions I find exciting:

  • 🔄 Hebbian learning: "Neurons that fire together, wire together" — can we integrate this?
  • ⚡ Spiking neural networks: Temporal dynamics like real neurons
  • 😴 Sleep and memory consolidation: What if models need "rest" to generalize?
  • 🎯 Attention as selective processing: Our brains don't process everything equally
4/4

I don't think the goal is to perfectly replicate brains—evolution optimized for different constraints than we have.

But I do think there's wisdom in biology we haven't fully tapped. The brain learns continuously, efficiently, and from remarkably little data compared to our models.

Making AI more "human" isn't about consciousness—it's about learning efficiency, adaptability, and robustness.

The Beautiful Game, Quantified

Football and tennis through the lens of data science. Where passion meets patterns...

1/3

There's something magical about sports analytics: it's where my ML skills meet my genuine passion. ⚽🎾

When I watch a match, I can't help but see it as a complex system—passes as edges in a graph, player positions as time series, tactical decisions as sequential choices.

2/3

Some questions I love exploring:

  • 🎾 Can we predict serve patterns from historical data and in-match adjustments?
  • ⚽ What makes a pressing system effective? (Hint: it's about network disruption)
  • 📊 How do we quantify "chemistry" between players?
  • 🔮 Can we identify undervalued players before the market does?
3/3

The fun part? Sports data is relatively clean, outcomes are measurable, and the feedback loop is fast.

It's a perfect playground for ML experimentation. And if my models can help my favorite team win? That's just a bonus. 😄

Lessons from the Trenches

Things I wish I knew earlier about building ML systems in the real world...

1/3

Lesson #1: Data quality > Model complexity

I've seen teams spend months on architecture tweaks when the real problem was noisy labels. A simple model on clean data often beats a complex model on messy data.

Before reaching for the latest paper, ask: "Have I really understood my data?"

2/3

Lesson #2: Your training pipeline is your product

Models are temporary. The pipeline that produces them is permanent. Invest in reproducibility, versioning, and monitoring early.

Future you will thank present you when something breaks at 2 AM.

3/3

Lesson #3: Explain before you deploy

If you can't explain your model's decisions to a non-technical stakeholder, you're not ready to deploy it. This isn't just ethics—it's debugging.

The process of explaining often reveals edge cases you hadn't considered.
