Carrick, AFCON, and the Question of Genuine Progress

Manchester United sacked Ruben Amorim in January, appointed Michael Carrick, and immediately looked transformed. But how much of that transformation is real?
Data from Understat & FBref  ·  Analysis via xG and Poisson expected points

When Michael Carrick took over Manchester United on 13 January 2026, the club had just sacked Ruben Amorim after 14 months. Results under Carrick improved sharply — but the timing of his appointment overlapped almost perfectly with the Africa Cup of Nations ending and United's two most influential players, Amad Diallo and Bryan Mbeumo, returning from international duty. This analysis uses match-level xG data and lineup records to separate what we can and cannot attribute to the new manager.

Setting the Scene

Three events defined United's season around the turn of 2026. Amorim was sacked on 5 January after a run of poor results and a public falling-out with club leadership. AFCON 2025 ran from 21 December to 18 January, taking Amad Diallo (Ivory Coast) and Bryan Mbeumo (Cameroon) out of the squad for five Premier League matches — four under Amorim, one under caretaker Darren Fletcher. And Carrick arrived on 13 January, inheriting a squad that was about to get two of its best players back.

The question the data needs to answer is whether Carrick's improved results reflect better management, or whether United were always going to look better once the AFCON players returned — whoever was in the dugout.

The Points Picture — and What Lies Beneath It

On raw points, the contrast is stark. Under Amorim, United averaged 1.55 points per game across 20 matches. Under Carrick, that rises to 2.17 points per game across 12 matches. But points alone conflate performance quality with result luck.

Amorim's actual points (31) tracked his deserved points (32.9) almost exactly — he was getting roughly what his performances merited. Carrick's 26 actual points from 12 games, however, represent a massive over-performance on his xPts of 18.6. That is a +7.4 point gap in just 12 matches — the kind of number that typically regresses over a full season.

Did AFCON Hurt Amorim?

The AFCON window cost United four league matches under Amorim without Amad Diallo and Bryan Mbeumo. Looking at those matches in isolation, the drop in performance quality is significant.

With a full squad, Amorim's United generated 1.71 xPts per game — a solid number that reflects a team creating and conceding chances at reasonable rates. Without Amad and Mbeumo, that drops to 1.39 xPts per game, with the xGD collapsing from +0.65 to +0.03. The team went from winning the xG battle to barely breaking even over those four matches.

Yes — AFCON hurt Amorim. But it is also true that the AFCON window coincided with the final weeks of a manager whose authority was already eroding. Whether a fully fit squad would have saved his job is a counterfactual the data cannot answer.

Is Carrick Actually Doing Better?

This is where the data delivers its most uncomfortable finding for the Carrick narrative. His xPts per game (1.55) is lower than Amorim's full-squad xPts per game (1.71). His xGD per game (+0.31) is also well below Amorim's full-squad figure (+0.65).

The headline finding: On underlying performance metrics — xPts/game and xGD/game — Amorim's United with a full squad was better than Carrick's United. Carrick's superior actual points come from a historically large over-performance (+7.4 pts vs xPts in 12 games) that is unlikely to sustain over a full season.

What We Can and Cannot Conclude

The data supports two reasonably firm conclusions and one important caveat.

AFCON hurt Amorim. The performance metrics drop clearly when Amad and Mbeumo were absent. Four matches is a small sample but the magnitude of the xGD shift is large enough to be meaningful.

Carrick's points are over-performing his xG by a large margin. A +7.4 point gap over 12 games is an outlier. This can reflect better game management, superior finishing, or variance — but it is not a stable signal of genuine improvement in underlying quality.

The confound cannot be resolved. Carrick took charge on 13 January. Amad and Mbeumo returned on 17 January. There is exactly one Carrick match without them — the 2-0 win over Manchester City — and zero Amorim matches after their return. The data cannot separate the manager effect from the player availability effect, and anyone claiming otherwise is overstating what 12 matches of evidence can tell us.

Methodology & Data Sources

Match-level xG data (home xG, away xG, scores, dates) sourced from Understat via the soccerdata Python library. Player lineup data (starter/bench status, minutes played) sourced from FBref via soccerdata. All 33 completed Premier League matches involving Manchester United in 2025/26 are included through the most recent gameweek.

Expected Points (xPts) are computed using an independent Poisson model: goals scored by each team are assumed to follow a Poisson distribution with mean equal to that team's xG. The probability of winning, drawing, and losing is derived by summing over all scoreline combinations up to 10 goals per side (capturing >99.9% of probability mass). xPts = 3 × P(win) + 1 × P(draw).

AFCON availability flags are derived from the FBref lineup data: a player is marked absent only if they have no recorded minutes in a match that falls within the AFCON 2025 window (21 Dec 2025 – 18 Jan 2026). Non-AFCON absences (injury, rotation) are not flagged. Only Amad Diallo and Bryan Mbeumo are tracked — Noussair Mazraoui was excluded as his impact was judged less analytically decisive.

Manager periods: Amorim (to 5 Jan 2026), Fletcher (5–13 Jan 2026), Carrick (13 Jan 2026 onwards). Fletcher's single match is excluded from sub-period comparisons due to insufficient sample size.
